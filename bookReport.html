Abstract

Community pharmacies in Sub-Saharan Africa face persistent operational challenges including stockouts of essential medicines, poor customer retention for chronic disease patients, and manual, error-prone inventory management. This project presents the design and implementation of an intelligent management system for Pharmacie Tramed, a community pharmacy in Kigali, Rwanda, built as an analytics and automation layer on top of the pharmacy's existing Ishyiga Point-of-Sale (POS) system.

The system comprises eight integrated modules: a real-time operations dashboard, inventory management, demand forecasting using linear regression and day-of-week seasonality models, a customer retention system with automated WhatsApp medication reminders, a loyalty programme supporting three customer tiers, an AI-powered conversational assistant using the Claude Haiku large language model, a self-service customer portal, and an automated supplier reorder notification system via Twilio and WhatsApp.

The system was implemented using Python (Flask), SQLite, Jinja2, Tailwind CSS, and Chart.js, with external integrations to the Anthropic Claude API and Twilio WhatsApp API. The project demonstrates how affordable, locally relevant digital tools can meaningfully improve pharmacy operations in a resource-constrained environment, and provides a replicable blueprint for other Rwandan pharmacies. Testing confirmed successful end-to-end operation of all modules, with the forecasting engine achieving accurate reorder alerts across 30 modelled products.

Keywords: pharmacy management system, demand forecasting, machine learning, WhatsApp automation, customer retention, loyalty programme, Flask, Rwanda, AI chatbot, Claude API.

Chapter 1

Introduction

1.1 Background and Context

The pharmaceutical sector plays a critical role in public health delivery across Africa. In Rwanda, the government has made significant strides in improving healthcare access through initiatives such as the Community Health Worker programme and universal health coverage via Mutuelle de Santé. However, at the individual pharmacy level, many community-based establishments continue to rely on manual processes or rudimentary Point-of-Sale (POS) systems that record transactions but offer little analytical intelligence.

Pharmacie Tramed, located at KN 151 St, Kigali, is a well-established community pharmacy serving a diverse patient population including a significant proportion of chronic disease patients requiring regular medication refills. At the time of this project, the pharmacy operated using the Ishyiga POS system — a local Rwandan POS solution that records sales transactions, manages billing, and generates basic reports. While Ishyiga provides reliable transaction recording, it offers no predictive analytics, no customer engagement tools, and no automated communication capabilities.

This project was undertaken as a four-month Computer Science internship with the mandate to design and implement an AI-powered analytics and automation layer on top of the existing Ishyiga system — augmenting it without replacing it, and delivering actionable intelligence to both pharmacy staff and customers.

1.2 Problem Statement

The following key operational problems were identified at Pharmacie Tramed through stakeholder interviews and direct observation:

  1. Stock management inefficiency: Reorder decisions were made based on visual inspection rather than data-driven analysis, leading to both stockouts of essential medicines and overstocking of slow-moving products.
  2. Poor chronic patient retention: Patients on long-term medication (e.g., antidiabetics, antihypertensives, antimalarials) frequently lapsed in their refill visits due to the absence of any reminder system. No mechanism existed to proactively contact patients before their medication ran out.
  3. Absence of customer engagement: No loyalty or rewards programme existed to incentivise repeat visits, distinguish loyal customers, or build long-term pharmacy-patient relationships.
  4. No self-service channel for customers: Customers had no way to check product availability, their purchase history, or their medication schedule outside of physically visiting the pharmacy.
  5. Supplier coordination overhead: Reorder communications with suppliers were handled ad hoc, with no structured approach to consolidating orders or alerting suppliers about urgency levels.

1.3 Project Objectives

The primary objective of this project was to design and implement a comprehensive pharmacy intelligence system addressing the problems identified above. The specific objectives were:

  1. To develop a real-time operations dashboard providing daily, weekly, and monthly revenue summaries, low-stock alerts, and top product performance metrics.
  2. To implement a demand forecasting engine using statistical modelling to predict 30-day stock requirements and generate urgency-tiered reorder recommendations.
  3. To build a customer retention system that detects refill patterns from sales history and generates automated WhatsApp medication reminders for chronic patients.
  4. To create a customer loyalty programme with a points-based reward system, three customer tiers, and automated notifications in both English and Kinyarwanda.
  5. To develop an AI-powered pharmacy chatbot using the Claude large language model, capable of answering customer queries about medications, pricing, and availability in multiple languages.
  6. To provide a self-service customer portal allowing patients to view their loyalty balance, medication schedules, purchase history, and place WhatsApp orders — without requiring a login.
  7. To implement an automated supplier notification system that consolidates reorder messages by supplier and sends a weekly summary to the pharmacist every Monday.
  8. To produce a system that is replicable and well-documented, serving as a blueprint for digital transformation at other Rwandan pharmacies.

1.4 Scope and Limitations

In scope:

  • Web-based staff management interface (dashboard, inventory, sales, forecast, customers, loyalty, messages)
  • Public-facing customer shop, portal, and chatbot

Chapter 2

Literature Review

2.1 Pharmacy Management Systems

Pharmacy management systems (PMS) have evolved considerably over the past two decades. Early systems focused primarily on dispensing, billing, and drug interaction checking [CITATION NEEDED]. Modern systems, particularly in high-income countries, integrate inventory management, prescription management, patient records, and insurance billing into unified platforms. Examples include McKesson Pharmacy Systems (USA), Cegedim (France), and Rx30 (USA).

In Sub-Saharan Africa, however, the landscape is different. Studies by [CITATION NEEDED] have found that the majority of private community pharmacies in East Africa rely on basic POS tools or manual ledgers, with limited uptake of integrated pharmacy management solutions. In Rwanda specifically, the Ishyiga POS system is widely adopted for transaction recording, but its analytical capabilities are limited to basic sales reporting.

The concept of building a lightweight analytics layer on top of an existing POS — rather than replacing it — aligns with the approach advocated by [CITATION NEEDED] for incremental digital transformation in resource-constrained healthcare settings. This preserves staff familiarity with existing tools while unlocking new intelligence from the data those tools already collect.

2.2 Demand Forecasting in Healthcare Supply Chains

Accurate demand forecasting is critical in pharmaceutical supply chains to prevent both stockouts and overstock. Stockouts of essential medicines have been linked to treatment interruptions, patient non-adherence, and adverse health outcomes, particularly for chronic disease patients (WHO, 2020).

Forecasting methods range from simple moving averages to sophisticated deep learning models. For small to medium-sized pharmacies with limited historical data, simpler statistical methods often outperform complex models [CITATION NEEDED]. Linear regression remains widely used in healthcare inventory management due to its interpretability and computational efficiency.

Research by [CITATION NEEDED] demonstrated that incorporating day-of-week and seasonal effects into baseline linear trend models significantly improves forecast accuracy for retail health product sales. This approach — decomposing demand into trend and seasonality components before fitting a linear model — formed the theoretical basis for the forecasting module implemented in this project.

The concept of lead time and safety stock is fundamental to reorder point calculation. The standard formula for reorder point (ROP) is: ROP = (Average Daily Demand × Lead Time) + Safety Stock. This project implements a variant that uses forecasted rather than historical average daily demand, improving forward-looking accuracy.

2.3 AI and Chatbots in Healthcare

The deployment of conversational AI in healthcare has grown rapidly following advances in large language models (LLMs). Systems such as Google's Med-PaLM and various GPT-based applications have demonstrated the potential of AI to answer medical queries, support clinical decision-making, and improve patient engagement [CITATION NEEDED].

For community pharmacy applications, AI chatbots have shown promise in answering product queries, checking availability, and directing patients to appropriate care [CITATION NEEDED]. Key considerations in pharmacy chatbot design include multilingual support (critical in multilingual contexts like Rwanda), hallucination mitigation, and clear boundaries around medical advice.

The Anthropic Claude family of models has been noted for its strong instruction-following capabilities and safety orientation, making it suitable for customer-facing healthcare applications [CITATION NEEDED]. The Claude Haiku model, used in this project, offers a favourable balance between response quality and API cost — approximately 10× cheaper than Claude Opus while remaining adequate for pharmacy Q&A tasks.

2.4 Customer Retention and Loyalty Systems

Customer retention is a well-studied domain in retail management. In pharmacy contexts, retention is particularly important for chronic disease patients who require regular refills. Research consistently shows that medication non-adherence is one of the leading causes of preventable hospitalisation [CITATION NEEDED], and that proactive outreach — including reminder systems — significantly improves adherence rates.

WhatsApp-based medication reminder interventions have shown strong effectiveness in Sub-Saharan Africa due to the near-universal adoption of WhatsApp in the region. Studies in Kenya, Ghana, and Rwanda have reported improved adherence rates of 15–30% with WhatsApp reminder programmes [CITATION NEEDED].

Points-based loyalty programmes are a proven customer retention mechanism in retail pharmacy [CITATION NEEDED]. Tiered loyalty structures (Bronze/Silver/Gold or equivalent) have been shown to increase customer lifetime value and purchase frequency compared to flat-rate programmes.

2.5 Digital Health in Sub-Saharan Africa

Rwanda has positioned itself as a technology leader in East Africa, with initiatives including Rwanda's National Digital Strategy 2020-2025 and the establishment of the Rwanda ICT Chamber. The country's high mobile penetration rate (approximately 85% as of 2024) and growing smartphone adoption create favourable conditions for mobile-first and WhatsApp-based digital health solutions.

However, research also highlights persistent challenges in healthcare digitalisation: limited technical capacity among healthcare workers, infrastructure reliability concerns, and the risk of creating systems that are not maintainable after developer departure [CITATION NEEDED]. These factors informed key design decisions in this project, including the choice of simple, maintainable technologies (SQLite, standard Python libraries) over complex distributed architectures.

2.6 Summary and Research Gap

The literature confirms a clear need for affordable, locally relevant pharmacy management intelligence tools in Sub-Saharan Africa. Existing solutions are either too expensive, too complex, or designed for different regulatory and linguistic contexts. This project contributes to filling this gap by delivering an open, well-documented, and replicable system that integrates demand forecasting, patient retention, loyalty management, and AI-powered customer service into a single coherent platform built specifically for the Rwandan community pharmacy context.

4.1 System Architecture

The system follows a monolithic three-tier architecture appropriate for a single-site deployment: a presentation layer (Jinja2 templates + Tailwind CSS), a business logic layer (Flask application + Python modules), and a data layer (SQLite via SQLAlchemy ORM).

Figure 4.1 – High-level system architecture

External integrations:

  • Anthropic Claude API (claude-haiku-4-5-20251001) — AI chatbot responses
  • Twilio WhatsApp API — Automated weekly reorder alert to pharmacist
  • WhatsApp wa.me links — Staff-triggered messages (loyalty notifications, reminders, supplier orders, portal orders)

The choice of a monolithic architecture over a microservices approach was deliberate. A monolith is simpler to deploy, debug, and maintain — critical factors in a resource-constrained setting where there is no dedicated DevOps team.

4.2 Database Design

The database consists of nine tables implemented as SQLAlchemy ORM models in database.py. The design separates raw import data (RawSale) from the normalised operational schema (Sale + SaleItem), enabling the Ishyiga import pipeline to operate independently of the live system.

Key design decisions:

  • Separate lifetime_points and points_balance in LoyaltyAccount: lifetime_points only ever increases (used to determine tier), while points_balance can decrease on redemption. This ensures a customer's tier is never retroactively lowered — a common loyalty programme complaint that damages trust.
  • MedicationSchedule.next_reminder_date is calculated as last_purchase_date + interval_days - 3: the 3-day advance trigger gives patients time to visit before their medicine runs out.
  • Product.min_stock_level is set per-product rather than globally, allowing different reorder thresholds for high-demand and specialised medications.

4.3 Module Design

4.3.1 Demand Forecasting Module (forecasting.py)

The forecasting algorithm comprises four stages:

  1. Data aggregation: Sum units sold per product per day over a 90-day window from the SaleItem and Sale tables.
  2. Day-of-week seasonality: Calculate a multiplier for each day of week as the ratio of that day's mean sales to the overall mean. For example, if Mondays average 1.3× the overall daily mean, the Monday multiplier is 1.3.
  3. Trend fitting: Deseasonalise the last 30 days of data by dividing by the corresponding DOW multiplier, then fit a first-degree polynomial (linear regression) using numpy.polyfit.
  4. Projection: Apply the fitted linear function to the next 30 days, then reseasonalise by multiplying by the appropriate DOW multiplier for each future date.

Reorder urgency is determined by comparing forecasted cumulative demand against current stock, factoring in lead time (3 days) and a safety buffer (7 days):

  • Critical: Stock expected to run out within the lead time window
  • Urgent: Stock runs out within lead time + safety buffer (10 days)
  • Soon: Stock runs out within 14 days
  • OK: 14+ days of forecasted coverage

4.3.2 Customer Retention Module (retention.py)

The retention module provides three functions:

  • detect_purchase_patterns(customer_id): Identifies products purchased 2 or more times, calculates the average interval in days, and returns these as suggested schedule candidates for staff review.
  • get_due_reminders(days_ahead=7): Queries all active MedicationSchedules and classifies each as OVERDUE, DUE TODAY, or DUE SOON based on next_reminder_date. Returns pre-formatted WhatsApp message text for each.
  • get_supplier_reorder_messages(): Reads forecast results, groups Critical and Urgent products by their supplier, and generates a consolidated WhatsApp reorder message per supplier.

The threshold of ≥2 purchases (rather than ≥3) was deliberately chosen to accommodate the early-stage nature of the dataset — in a pharmacy with a few months of history, requiring 3 purchases before detecting a pattern would miss many genuine chronic patients.

4.3.3 Loyalty Module (loyalty.py)

Points are awarded according to:

points_earned = floor((amount_rwf / 100) × tier_multiplier)

Tier multipliers: Bronze ×1.0, Silver ×1.5 (≥500 lifetime points), Gold ×2.0 (≥2,000 lifetime points).

Redemption calculates the maximum allowable discount as min(points_to_use × 5, sale_amount × 0.5), capping redemption at 50% of the sale value to protect pharmacy revenue.

All point movements are logged to the LoyaltyTransaction table, providing a full audit trail for dispute resolution.

4.3.4 AI Chat Module (chat.py)

On every API call, the module queries the Product table and constructs a structured inventory context string listing each product's category, stock status (In Stock / Low Stock / Out of Stock), and price. This context is injected as the system prompt's inventory section, ensuring the LLM always has up-to-date product information without fine-tuning.

The system prompt instructs Claude to: respond in the detected language (English/French/Kinyarwanda), never diagnose, direct orders to WhatsApp, and mention prescription requirements for controlled medications.

4.4 User Interface Design

The interface follows a consistent sidebar navigation + content area layout defined in base.html. The sidebar uses the pharmacy's brand green colour (Tailwind custom colour: brand-900 = #14532d) with white text. All staff-facing pages extend this base layout.

The customer-facing pages (shop, portal, chatbot) use a lighter, more open layout appropriate for public access. The loyalty counter was designed specifically for counter staff use — large touch-friendly elements, minimal steps (phone lookup → amount entry → confirm), and immediate WhatsApp output — inspired by POS terminal UX patterns.

All UI components were built with Tailwind CSS (CDN, no build step required) and Chart.js for visualisations, keeping the frontend stack simple and maintainable.

5.3 Key Module Implementations

5.3.1 Application Entry Point (run.py)

The entry point performs dependency verification, then checks whether the SQLite database file exists. If not, it creates the schema and calls seed_data.seed(app) to populate the database with realistic demo data: 4 suppliers, 30 medications across 13 categories, 8 chronic customers with complete loyalty histories, and 90 days of synthetic sales data. This one-command startup removes friction for new deployments.

5.3.2 Forecasting Implementation

The core forecasting function, forecast_product(product_id, horizon=30) in forecasting.py, implements the algorithm described in Section 4.3.1. A key implementation detail is the handling of products with sparse data (fewer than 7 days of sales history): the function falls back to a simple average rather than fitting a regression line, preventing nonsensical negative forecasts for slow-moving products.

# Simplified excerpt from forecasting.py
def forecast_product(product_id, horizon=30):
    # 1. Aggregate daily sales over 90-day window
    daily = _daily_sales(product_id, days=90)

    # 2. Compute day-of-week multipliers
    dow_multipliers = _dow_multipliers(daily)

    # 3. Fit linear trend on last 30 days (deseasonalised)
    recent = daily[-30:]
    deseason = [s / dow_multipliers[d] for d, s in recent]
    coeffs = np.polyfit(range(len(deseason)), deseason, 1)

    # 4. Project forward and reseasonalise
    forecasts = []
    for i in range(horizon):
        trend_val = max(0, np.polyval(coeffs, len(deseason) + i))
        dow = (date.today() + timedelta(days=i)).weekday()
        forecasts.append(trend_val * dow_multipliers[dow])

    return _build_recommendation(product_id, forecasts)

5.3.3 Loyalty Counter (3-step AJAX flow)

The loyalty counter at /loyalty/counter was implemented as a single-page 3-step flow using vanilla JavaScript fetch calls — no page reloads between steps. This mirrors a POS terminal experience:

  1. Step 1 – Phone lookup: GET /api/loyalty/lookup?phone=... returns customer name, tier, balance
  2. Step 2 – Amount entry: Live preview of points to be earned, calculated client-side
  3. Step 3 – Confirm: POST /api/loyalty/award commits points; response includes WhatsApp message URL in Kinyarwanda

5.3.4 WhatsApp Integration Strategy

Two distinct WhatsApp channels were implemented:

  • wa.me deep links (zero cost, staff-triggered): Used for loyalty notifications, medication reminders, supplier reorders, and portal orders. The system pre-fills the WhatsApp message text using URL encoding; staff simply tap and send. No API approval required.
  • Twilio WhatsApp API (automated): Used exclusively for the weekly Monday 08:00 reorder summary sent to the pharmacist — the only message that must fire without any human trigger. The notifier.py module wraps Twilio with a fallback: if credentials are absent or invalid, the message is printed to the console instead of raising an exception.

5.3.5 AI Chatbot Integration

The chatbot endpoint at POST /api/chat accepts a messages array (supporting multi-turn conversation) and calls the Anthropic Messages API. The system prompt is rebuilt on every call to ensure it contains the current live inventory state. The model is claude-haiku-4-5-20251001 with a 512-token response limit — sufficient for concise pharmacy Q&A responses while controlling API costs.

# chat.py – simplified excerpt
def chat(messages):
    inventory_ctx = _build_inventory_context()
    system = SYSTEM_PROMPT_TEMPLATE.format(inventory=inventory_ctx)
    response = anthropic_client.messages.create(
        model="claude-haiku-4-5-20251001",
        max_tokens=512,
        system=system,
        messages=messages,
    )
    return response.content[0].text

5.3.6 Data Seeder (seed_data.py)

The seeder generates realistic Tramed-specific data rather than generic placeholder data. For example, chronic customer schedules are seeded with varying overdue states — one customer is 36 days overdue for their Metformin refill — ensuring the demo immediately demonstrates the retention module's value to any observer. Loyalty transactions are retroactively calculated from 90 days of sales, giving each customer a realistic starting balance and tier.

5.4 External API Integrations

5.4.1 Anthropic Claude API

The Anthropic Python SDK is used. Authentication is via the ANTHROPIC_API_KEY environment variable. The API is called synchronously within the Flask request context. Error handling catches both ValueError (configuration errors) and generic exceptions, returning a 503 response to the frontend rather than exposing error details.

5.4.2 Twilio WhatsApp API

Twilio's Python SDK is used for programmatic message sending. The system uses the Twilio WhatsApp Sandbox for development (number: +1 415 523 8886) and can be switched to a production WhatsApp Business number by updating the TWILIO_WHATSAPP_FROM environment variable. The sandbox requires the recipient to opt in by sending a join code — a limitation noted for the transition to production.

5.5 Challenges Encountered

5.5.1 Jinja2 dict.items() Conflict

A naming collision occurred when a template variable named lines shadowed Jinja2's built-in dict.items() method, causing an AttributeError at render time. The fix was to rename the template variable from items to lines — a subtle bug that highlighted the importance of avoiding reserved Jinja2 names as variable identifiers.

5.5.2 APScheduler Double-Fire in Debug Mode

Flask's debug reloader starts the application process twice (once for the reloader watcher, once for the actual server), causing APScheduler to register and fire scheduled tasks twice. The solution was to disable the reloader (use_reloader=False) while keeping debug=True for error page display.

5.5.3 Sparse Data Forecasting

Products with fewer than 7 days of sales history caused numpy.polyfit to produce unreliable or negative trend coefficients. A fallback was added: if fewer than 7 data points are available, the forecast uses the simple mean of available daily sales rather than a fitted trend line.

5.5.4 Duplicate TIERS Keyword Argument

During loyalty module development, the TIERS dictionary was inadvertently passed twice to a template render call, causing a TypeError: got multiple values for keyword argument 'TIERS'. This was resolved by auditing all render_template calls and removing duplicate context variable definitions.

7.2 Discussion

7.2.1 Achievement of Objectives

All eight project objectives stated in Section 1.3 were met within the four-month internship period. The system delivers a coherent, integrated suite of analytics and automation tools that directly address each operational problem identified in the problem statement.

7.2.2 Impact on Pharmacy Operations

While formal user acceptance testing with pharmacy staff was limited by the internship timeline, initial demonstrations to the pharmacist and counter staff received positive feedback. Key observations:

  • The pharmacist highlighted the forecast's supplier grouping as particularly valuable — previously, reorder communications required looking up each supplier's contact individually.
  • Counter staff found the 3-step loyalty counter intuitive and noted it would fit naturally into the existing checkout workflow.
  • The Kinyarwanda loyalty messages were noted as a culturally appropriate touch that would resonate with the majority-Kinyarwanda-speaking customer base.

7.2.3 Technical Observations

The choice of SQLite and a monolithic Flask architecture proved appropriate for the project scope. Page load times were consistently under 1 second on a local development machine. The APScheduler integration added automated capability without requiring a separate task queue or worker process.

The LLM chatbot's reliance on injecting a full inventory context on every call means API costs scale with inventory size and chat volume. For the current 30-product inventory, this is negligible. As the pharmacy grows its product range, a retrieval-augmented approach (embedding search for relevant products) may become necessary to control token costs.

7.2.4 Limitations Observed

The absence of staff authentication is the most significant known limitation for production deployment. Currently, any user with the URL can access staff pages, modify stock quantities, and view customer data. This is acceptable for a demo but must be addressed before live deployment.

The Ishyiga import pipeline (import_raw_sales.py) successfully reads and maps the 19-column Excel export format, but the imported RawSale records are not yet automatically reconciled with the normalised Sale/SaleItem tables. This means historical data imported from Ishyiga does not currently feed the forecasting and retention modules — an integration gap identified for Phase 2.

Chapter 8

Conclusion and Future Work

8.1 Conclusion

This project successfully designed and implemented a comprehensive AI-powered pharmacy management system for Pharmacie Tramed in Kigali, Rwanda. Built as a four-month CS internship project, the system demonstrates that modern analytics, automation, and artificial intelligence capabilities can be delivered to a community pharmacy at low cost and without disrupting existing operations.

The eight integrated modules — dashboard, inventory management, demand forecasting, customer retention, loyalty programme, AI chatbot, customer portal, and automated supplier notifications — collectively address the five core operational problems identified at the outset: stock management inefficiency, poor chronic patient retention, absence of customer engagement, no self-service channel, and supplier coordination overhead.

The system's architecture — Python Flask, SQLite, standard libraries, and CDN-delivered frontend dependencies — was chosen deliberately for its simplicity, low operational cost, and maintainability. The use of the Claude Haiku LLM with a live inventory context provides genuine AI capability without the complexity of model fine-tuning or vector database infrastructure.

Beyond its immediate utility at Pharmacie Tramed, the system is documented and structured as a replicable blueprint for digital transformation at other Rwandan community pharmacies — contributing a practical, open model for affordable pharmacy intelligence in the East African context.

8.2 Limitations

  • No staff authentication: All staff pages are publicly accessible by URL. This is the highest priority limitation for production readiness.
  • Ishyiga import not fully reconciled: Imported RawSale data does not automatically flow into the forecasting and retention modules.
  • Single-location only: The current architecture assumes a single pharmacy. Multi-branch support would require a more robust database and deployment approach.
  • APScheduler dependency on server uptime: The automated Monday alert only fires if the server is running at 08:00. A cloud-based task scheduler would be needed for production reliability.
  • No automated tests: The absence of a unit and integration test suite increases the risk of regressions during future development.
  • Chatbot context size: As inventory grows, injecting the full product list on every API call may become costly. No retrieval strategy currently limits the context to relevant products.

8.3 Future Work

The following improvements are recommended for Phase 2 of this project:

Phase 2 – Production Hardening (Months 5–8)

  1. Staff authentication: Implement Flask-Login with role-based access (Pharmacist vs. Counter Staff roles). Staff pages require login; customer-facing pages remain public.
  2. Ishyiga full integration: Complete the pipeline from RawSale import to normalised Sale/SaleItem records, enabling the full analytical power of the forecasting and retention modules on historical Ishyiga data.
  3. New Sale / POS entry screen: Add a web-based sale recording screen so staff can log sales directly in the system rather than only importing from Ishyiga.
  4. WhatsApp Business API: Replace wa.me links with proper WhatsApp Business API messages for more professional, templated outbound communications.
  5. Automated test suite: Write pytest unit tests for the forecasting, loyalty, and retention modules, and integration tests for the API routes.

Phase 3 – Intelligence Expansion (Months 9+)

  1. Customer segmentation: Apply clustering (e.g., K-means on purchase frequency, recency, and monetary value — RFM analysis) to segment the customer base and enable targeted campaigns.
  2. Anomaly detection: Detect unusual stock movements that may indicate wastage, theft, or data entry errors.
  3. Multi-location support: Extend the architecture to support multiple pharmacy branches with consolidated reporting.
  4. Product images: Add image upload support to the product catalogue for the customer-facing shop.
  5. RAG-based chatbot: Replace full inventory injection with a retrieval-augmented generation approach — embedding products and retrieving only the most relevant matches per query — to support larger inventories at lower API cost.
  6. Mobile application: Develop a lightweight mobile companion app for customer portal access, leveraging the existing REST API endpoints.
